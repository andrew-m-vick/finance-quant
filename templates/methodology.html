Methodology

The assumptions, the math, and what these models can and can't tell you.

1. The Stress Test

The stress test is a heuristic scorecard, not a statistical model. It runs the user's balance sheet through five adverse scenarios and grades the response on a weighted rubric.

1a. Scenarios

  • Job loss runway. Months of fixed expenses plus 50% of variable expenses that liquid savings can cover with zero income. Thresholds: 12+ months strong, 6–12 adequate, 3–6 weak, <3 critical. The 12-month bar is aggressive by mainstream advice (3–6 months is the usual guidance), but it's the level at which you can realistically survive a bad labor market for a technical role without forced fire sales.
  • 30% market drawdown. Applies a 30% loss to invested assets. This approximates the S&P 500's behavior in 2008 (−37%), 2020 COVID crash (−34% intraday), and 2022 tech-led correction (−25%). Marked informational because you can't "fix" being invested — the goal is to confirm your liquid cushion exists outside the drawdown.
  • $5K emergency expense. A round-number shock that approximates a medical deductible, a transmission, or a family obligation. Measures whether one bad week empties your cushion.
  • Debt service ratio. Monthly debt payments ÷ monthly income. Thresholds mirror mortgage-underwriting bands: <15% strong, <28% adequate (the front-end DTI ceiling used by many lenders), <40% weak (back-end DTI ceiling), ≥40% critical.
  • 5% inflation for 2 years. Compounds expenses at 5% annually while growing income at 2% — modeling the real-world lag between prices and wages. This was the lived experience of 2021–2023, where CPI ran ~5–9% and wage growth trailed.

1b. Scoring

Each scenario maps to a 0–100 severity score. The final resilience score is a weighted average: 

score = 0.35·runway + 0.20·emergency + 0.20·DTI + 0.15·inflation + 0.10·drawdown

Job loss gets the heaviest weight because it's the most common catastrophic financial event for working-age adults. Market drawdown gets the lowest because, unlike the others, you don't act on it — you wait.

2. The Monte Carlo Retirement Simulator

2a. Model

Portfolio balance is propagated year-by-year with contributions at the start of each year and stochastic returns drawn from a log-normal distribution: 

B_{t+1} = (B_t + C) · exp(Z_t)
Z_t ~ Normal(ln(1+μ) − σ²/2, σ²)

Where B is balance, C is annual contribution, μ is the expected arithmetic return (i.e. the average of annual (1+R)−1 outcomes), and σ is the standard deviation of log-returns. Drawing Z_t from this shifted distribution makes E[1 + R] = 1 + μ — so the long-run expected portfolio value compounds at (1+μ)^T, not exp(μT). (Those differ noticeably over decades.)

2b. Why geometric, not arithmetic, returns

This is the most common mistake in retirement projections. If you project growth at the arithmetic mean return μ, you will systematically overstate outcomes. The reason is that compounding is multiplicative, not additive: a +50% year followed by a −50% year is not flat — it's −25%.

The relationship between the arithmetic mean μ and the geometric mean g of log-normal returns is: 

g ≈ μ − σ²/2

So for an equity portfolio with μ = 10% and σ = 16%, the geometric return is ≈ 8.7%. Over 40 years, that 1.3% drag compounds to roughly a 40% reduction in terminal wealth. The engine draws log-returns from Normal(μ − σ²/2, σ²) so that the expected ending balance is consistent with μ while individual paths still capture volatility drag.

2c. Sequence of returns risk

Two retirees with identical average returns can end up in wildly different places depending on when the bad years hit. Bad years early — when the portfolio is still small and contributions dominate — are survivable. Bad years late — when the portfolio is large and you are about to or already drawing down — are not.

This simulator captures sequence risk implicitly: each of the 10,000 paths has a unique return sequence. The 10th-percentile path is almost always a path where the worst years clustered near the end. That is why the fan chart fans out dramatically in the final decade — outcomes diverge based on sequence, not just mean.

2d. Inflation and real vs. nominal

Nominal dollars at retirement are deflated at the user's inflation rate to produce real (today's) dollars: 

real = nominal / (1 + i)^years

The real column of the percentiles table is the honest number — it represents purchasing power in today's terms. Nominal figures are larger and look more impressive, but they are quoted in dollars that buy less.

2e. The 4% rule context

A common way to convert a goal number into retirement income is the 4% safe-withdrawal rule (Bengen 1994, Trinity Study 1998): a retiree withdrawing 4% of their starting balance, adjusted for inflation, had a ≥95% historical success rate over 30-year horizons on a 60/40 portfolio. So a $2M target implies roughly $80,000/year of pretax inflation-adjusted income. The rule has well-documented weaknesses (it's backtested on US data, assumes a specific asset mix, and ignores sequence risk at the start of retirement) but it remains a useful sanity check.

3. What these models can't tell you

  • Fat tails. Both tools assume returns are roughly normal. Real markets have fatter tails — extreme events happen more often than a Gaussian predicts. The 10th-percentile outcome is probably optimistic about how bad the bad cases really are.
  • Stationary distributions. The simulator assumes μ and σ are constant across the horizon. Regimes change — interest rates, valuations, and correlations shift over decades. Recalibrate periodically.
  • Correlated shocks. The stress test treats scenarios independently, but in practice a job loss and a market drawdown often happen together (2008, 2020). Your true resilience is lower than any single scenario suggests.
  • Behavior. The biggest risk to any long-horizon plan is the investor selling at the bottom. No model captures that.
